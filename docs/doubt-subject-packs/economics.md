## Subject-specific guidance — Economics

**Top student traps in CBSE economics:**
- Confusing movement *along* a curve (caused by change in price) vs *shift* of a curve (caused by change in non-price factors like income, tastes, prices of other goods).
- Mixing income elasticity, price elasticity, and cross elasticity — sign matters: negative cross elasticity means complements, positive means substitutes.
- Wrong sign for elasticity (price elasticity of demand is always negative in pure form but is reported as absolute value in CBSE answers — clarify).
- Confusing GDP vs GNP vs NNP vs NDP. Add net factor income from abroad to convert GDP → GNP; subtract depreciation to convert gross → net.
- Mixing types of unemployment (frictional vs structural vs cyclical vs disguised vs seasonal).
- In microeconomics, confusing different cost concepts — fixed vs variable, average vs marginal, short-run vs long-run.

**Notation and presentation:**
- Graphs always need axis labels (Price on Y-axis, Quantity on X-axis is the CBSE convention for demand/supply diagrams).
- Curve labels (DD for demand, SS for supply, MC for marginal cost, MR for marginal revenue).
- Equilibrium point marked clearly with coordinates if known.
- Numerical answers with units: ₹, %, units of output, persons, etc.

**CBSE marking specifics:**
- 3-mark numerical / definitional: formula or definition + working + final answer with units.
- 4-mark theory: 4 points, each worth a mark; structure as numbered or short-paragraph.
- 6-mark essay: introduction + 4 substantive arguments + conclusion. Diagrams (where relevant) carry their own marks — usually 1-2 of the 6 are for the diagram alone.
- For graph questions: drawing the curves correctly is worth marks even before the explanation.
- Examples should be Indian context when possible — RBI, SEBI, NITI Aayog, agricultural sector specifics.

**Model honesty for this subject:**
- Do not fabricate specific Indian economic statistics — GDP growth rates, inflation rates, unemployment figures change yearly; if you give a number, flag it as "around X, please verify with a recent source."
- RBI / SEBI / NITI Aayog roles are stable — quote confidently.
- Policy changes (specific budget announcements, GST rate changes) — do not assume; if asked, defer to a recent source.
- Theory (Keynesian, Classical, Monetarist) is stable — quote the canonical positions confidently as CBSE presents them.
- For Indian Economic Development (grade 12 sometimes paired): five-year-plan content is stable up to the 12th plan (2012-17); after that, planning shifted to NITI Aayog framework.
