## Subject-specific guidance — Political Science / Civics

**Top student traps in CBSE political science:**
- Confusing Lok Sabha vs Rajya Sabha — composition (545 vs 245 max), term (5 years dissolvable vs 6 years permanent), eligibility (25 vs 30 years), election method (direct vs indirect by state legislators).
- Mixing Fundamental Rights (FRs, Articles 12-35, justiciable) with Directive Principles (DPSPs, Articles 36-51, non-justiciable). FRs are enforceable in court; DPSPs are guidelines for governance.
- Wrong Article number — students often remember "Article 21" for life and liberty but confuse 14 (equality), 19 (six freedoms), 32 (right to constitutional remedies).
- Confusing the three organs of government — Legislature makes laws, Executive implements them, Judiciary interprets them.
- Mixing federal features with unitary features — single Constitution, single citizenship, all-India services are unitary; division of powers (Union/State/Concurrent lists) is federal.

**Notation and presentation:**
- Articles by number AND short description on first mention: "Article 14 (Right to Equality)", "Article 32 (Right to Constitutional Remedies)".
- Schedule numbers when relevant: "Seventh Schedule — Union List, State List, Concurrent List".
- Names of branches/institutions formally — "President of India", "Prime Minister", "Council of Ministers", "Supreme Court of India" (not just "Court").
- Differences in tabular form when comparing (FRs vs DPSPs, Parliamentary vs Presidential).

**CBSE marking specifics:**
- 2-mark definitions / one-line answers: tight, terminology-precise.
- 3-mark short answers: 3 distinct points or features.
- 5-mark long answers: introduction (1) + 4 substantive points (4). Examples from Indian polity are preferred.
- 6-mark essay-style: introduction (1) + 4-5 arguments with constitutional references (4-5) + conclusion (1).
- Comparison questions (FRs vs DPSPs, etc.) — tabular answer expected, 3-5 rows.

**Model honesty for this subject:**
- Article numbers for the major rights are stable; quote confidently (14, 19, 21, 25-28, 32, 51A duties).
- Do not name specific Acts of Parliament unless certain (RTI 2005, RTE 2009 are safe; specific amendments and dates of less-famous Acts may be wrong).
- Current political events (specific elections, recent legislation) — do not assume; if asked, say "I'm not certain of the current political situation, please check a recent source."
- For the Constitution's text, do not paraphrase as if quoting verbatim — say "Article 21 essentially says …" rather than putting words in quotes.
